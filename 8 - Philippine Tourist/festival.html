ETAG
FESTIVAL

Discover the Etag Festival in the Philippines, a vibrant celebration of indigenous culture featuring traditional dances, music, and cuisine. Immerse yourself in the rich traditions of the region and experience the warmth and hospitality of the local community.

Background | Culture | Event

Background


The Etag Festival traces its roots back to the indigenous communities of the Cordillera region in the Philippines, where it has been celebrated for generations as a symbol of cultural identity and heritage. The festival's origins can be traced to the traditional practice of preserving meat through drying, a method that has been passed down through the ages as a way to ensure food security and sustainability in the mountainous terrain.


Historically, the Etag Festival served as a time for communities to come together and celebrate successful harvests, express gratitude to the spirits of the land, and honor their ancestors for their blessings. It was also an opportunity for people to showcase their culinary skills and creativity, as they prepared and shared their prized Etag delicacies with friends, family, and visitors. Over the years, the Etag Festival has evolved into a vibrant cultural celebration, featuring a wide array of activities and events that highlight the unique traditions and customs of the Cordillera people. From colorful parades and traditional dances to music performances and culinary showcases, the festival offers a diverse and immersive experience for participants and spectators alike.


Today, the Etag Festival continues to thrive as a cherished tradition in the Cordillera region, attracting visitors from near and far who come to experience the warmth, hospitality, and rich cultural heritage of the Cordillera people. It serves as a reminder of the enduring resilience and strength of the indigenous communities and their deep connection to the land they call home.

BELIEFS

During the festival, rituals and ceremonies are often conducted to honor these spirits and seek their blessings for prosperous future harvests. Offerings of food, beverages, and other symbolic items are made at sacred sites, accompanied by prayers and chants to express gratitude and devotion to these spiritual entities.


It is also common to believe that Etag, as a symbol of the earth's fertility and the abundance of the harvest, possesses special powers and protects those who consume it from illness and misfortune. Therefore, the preparation and consumption of Etag are often surrounded by special rituals and ancestral traditions passed down through generations.

TRADITION

The Etag Festival is celebrated to honor the indigenous culture and traditions of the Cordillera region, as well as to express gratitude for successful harvests and blessings from their ancestors. It serves as a time for communities to come together, strengthen bonds, and showcase their unique customs and heritage. Additionally, the festival highlights the importance of preserving and promoting indigenous practices, ensuring that future generations continue to appreciate and uphold their cultural identity.

MEN'S ATTIRE

The traditional clothing worn by me includes woven garments such as "wanes" (loincloths) or "g-strings" paired with intricately designed vests or jackets adorned with tribal patterns and embroidery. Accessories like headbands, beaded necklaces, and anklets are also common. Additionally, a headgear called "tapis" is also worn.

WOMEN'S ATTIRE

Women's traditional attire may consist of "tapis" (wrap-around skirts) or "tubular skirts" intricately woven with colorful patterns and symbols representing their cultural heritage. Blouses or "camisa" with intricate embroidery are often worn on top, complemented by shawls or scarves draped elegantly over the shoulders. Accessories such as beaded jewelry, hair ornaments, tikog, and woven bags complete the ensemble.

CULTURE

Discover the culture of the Etag Festival!

EVENT

The Etag Festival is a celebration deeply rooted in the cultural traditions of the Cordillera region in the Philippines. This vibrant event typically spans several days and is marked by a series of colorful and lively activities that showcase the unique heritage of the Cordillera people.


The festival kicks off with a grand parade, where participants don traditional attire adorned with intricate designs and vibrant colors. Traditional dances accompanied by rhythmic drumming and music fill the air, captivating spectators and participants alike.


Throughout the festival, there are various cultural performances, including indigenous music and dance showcases, storytelling sessions, and demonstrations of traditional crafts such as weaving and wood carving. Visitors have the opportunity to immerse themselves in the rich cultural tapestry of the Cordillera region and learn about the customs and traditions passed down through generations.

Etag, the traditional smoked meat featured prominently at the Etag Festival, is a culinary delight deeply ingrained in Cordillera cuisine. The preparation of Etag involves a meticulous process of marinating and smoking meat, typically pork or beef, over an open fire. This time-honored technique imbues the meat with a distinct smoky flavor and tender texture, making it a beloved delicacy among locals and visitors alike.


During the festival, Etag takes center stage as chefs and food artisans showcase various dishes highlighting this flavorful ingredient. From hearty stews to savory skewers and innovative appetizers, there is no shortage of creative ways to enjoy Etag. Visitors are encouraged to sample these culinary creations, experiencing firsthand the rich flavors and cultural significance of this traditional Cordillera fare.

Beyond the cultural and culinary highlights, the Etag Festival offers a diverse array of activities that cater to the interests of all attendees. Sports competitions, such as traditional games like "kadang-kadang" (wooden scooter racing) and "patok" (stick wrestling), showcase the athleticism and agility of local athletes.


Parades featuring colorful floats, street performers, and marching bands add to the festive atmosphere, while community activities like arts and crafts workshops, storytelling sessions, and cultural exhibits provide opportunities for hands-on learning and interactive experiences. These diverse offerings ensure that there is something for everyone to enjoy at the Etag Festival, fostering a sense of unity and camaraderie among participants and creating lasting memories for locals and visitors alike.

WHERE

The Etag Festival is celebrated across the Cordillera region of the Philippines, with towns like Bontoc, Sagada, Banaue, and Baguio City hosting vibrant festivities. In Bontoc, street parades and cultural performances characterize the event, while Sagada showcases traditional rituals. Banaue provides a stunning backdrop of rice terraces, and Baguio City offers street dances and art exhibitions. These diverse locations highlight the rich cultural heritage of the Cordillera people, attracting locals and tourists alike. With each town adding its unique flair, the Etag Festival offers a range of experiences for visitors to immerse themselves in Cordillera culture.

WHEN

The Etag Festival typically occurs during the harvest season, commonly in April or May, across various towns and municipalities within the Cordillera region of the Philippines. While specific dates may vary annually and between different locations, the festival consistently highlights the rich cultural heritage and traditions of the Cordillera people. It serves as a time for communities to come together, celebrate successful harvests, and share their cultural heritage with locals and visitors alike. The festive atmosphere, vibrant performances, and culinary delights make the Etag Festival a memorable experience for all who partake in the celebrations.

ADIVAY
FESTIVAL

Experience the vibrant Adivay Festival in the Philippines, showcasing indigenous culture through traditional dances, music, and cuisine. Dive into rich traditions and embrace local hospitality.

Background | Culture | Event

Background


The Adivay Festival has a rich history intertwined with the cultural beliefs and traditions of the Cordillera region. It originated from the collective desire of the Cordillerans to celebrate their unique heritage and strengthen the bonds among the various indigenous communities. The festival is deeply rooted in the indigenous beliefs and practices of the Cordilleran people, which emphasize harmony with nature, reverence for ancestral spirits, and respect for communal values.


One prominent aspect of the Adivay Festival is its focus on preserving and showcasing traditional rituals, dances, and music that have been passed down through generations. These cultural performances often reflect the spiritual beliefs and cosmology of the Cordilleran tribes, paying homage to deities, ancestors, and the natural world.


The festival also serves as a platform for indigenous artisans to display their craftsmanship, including intricate weaving, woodcarving, and metalwork. These traditional crafts hold deep cultural significance and are often imbued with symbolic meanings related to identity, status, and spirituality.

BELIEFS

The indigenous peoples of the Cordillera region ascribe deep spiritual and cultural significance to the Adivay Festival. For them, this celebration is more than just a cultural event; it is a sacred occasion that honors their ancestral traditions and beliefs.


One core belief underlying the Adivay Festival is the idea of reciprocity with nature and the spirit world. Indigenous communities in the Cordillera hold a profound respect for the environment and believe in maintaining a harmonious relationship with the natural world. The festival serves as a way to express gratitude to the spirits of the land for their bounty and blessings, while also seeking their continued protection and guidance.

TRADITION

The Adivay Festival in the Cordillera region embodies rich traditions, including traditional dances, music, and crafts, showcasing the cultural heritage of indigenous peoples. These festivities serve as a platform for spiritual rituals, honoring ancestors and nature spirits through offerings and ceremonies conducted by tribal elders.


Additionally, the festival fosters intertribal gatherings, promoting cultural exchange and unity among Cordillera communities. Through vibrant performances and artisanal displays, the festival preserves indigenous traditions while providing economic opportunities for local artisans. Overall, the Adivay Festival is a celebration of resilience, creativity, and cultural pride, embodying the spirit of Cordillera heritage.

MEN'S ATTIRE

During the Adivay Festival, men in the Cordillera region wear traditional attire reflecting their cultural identity. This includes handwoven bahags, embroidered shirts, and fabric hats, often adorned with silver or brass jewelry. These garments represent a deep cultural heritage and are integral to the festival's celebration of indigenous culture.

WOMEN'S ATTIRE

During the Adivay Festival, women in the Cordillera region adorn themselves in traditional attire that embodies their cultural heritage. This includes intricately woven skirts or dresses, embroidered blouses, and indigenous accessories such as beadwork or rattan belts. These garments showcase the rich cultural identity of Cordillera women and are integral to the festival's celebration of indigenous culture.

Pinikpikan is a traditional dish from the Cordillera region of the Philippines, renowned for its unique preparation method and distinctive flavor. The dish starts with a whole chicken, preferably native or free-range, which is carefully prepared for cooking. One of the key steps in pinikpikan's preparation is the ritualistic process known as "pikpik," where the chicken is gently beaten with a stick or blunt object. This practice is believed to tenderize the meat and enhance its flavor.


Pinikpikan is typically served hot and accompanied by various side dishes, such as rice, vegetables, and condiments. The flavorful broth produced during cooking is also a highlight of the dish, often enjoyed as a soup or used as a dipping sauce.

Cultural showcases feature vibrant parades, traditional dances, and musical performances, offering insight into the region's rich heritage. Culinary delights abound, with indigenous delicacies like pinikpikan and etag tantalizing taste buds. Artisanal exhibits showcase local crafts, textiles, and woodcarvings, while interactive workshops provide opportunities to learn traditional techniques.


Spiritual rituals and ceremonies conducted by indigenous elders offer glimpses into ancient beliefs and practices. Engaging with local communities fosters cultural exchange and understanding. The festival fosters a sense of unity and pride among Cordillera residents while inviting visitors to partake in the region's cultural tapestry. With its blend of tradition, artistry, and community spirit, the Adivay Festival promises a memorable and enriching experience for all who attend, forging connections and memories that endure long after the festivities have concluded.

WHERE

The Adivay Festival is a multi-provincial celebration in the Cordillera region of the Philippines, spanning Benguet, Ifugao, Mountain Province, and Kalinga. Each province hosts its version, showcasing unique cultural heritage. La Trinidad in Benguet features street parades and agricultural showcases. Banaue in Ifugao highlights traditional rituals and the iconic rice terraces. Bontoc, Mountain Province, offers indigenous traditions and culinary displays. Tabuk, Kalinga, presents traditional performances and outdoor activities. With each province adding its flair, the Adivay Festival provides diverse experiences for visitors, offering glimpses into the rich history, culture, and natural beauty of the Cordillera region.

WHEN

The Adivay Festival typically occurs annually, although exact dates may vary slightly depending on the province and local arrangements. Generally, the festival takes place during the latter part of the year, often in the months of November or December. This timing coincides with the harvest season in the Cordillera region, making it an opportune time to celebrate the bountiful harvest and showcase the cultural traditions and heritage of the indigenous peoples. Visitors can expect a vibrant and festive atmosphere filled with cultural performances, culinary delights, and community events, making it an ideal time to experience the unique charm of the Cordillera region.

PANAGBENGA
FESTIVAL

Experience the vibrant ambiance of Baguio's Panagbenga Festival, where captivating floral displays and cultural performances mesmerize visitors, offering an unforgettable celebration of the city's rich heritage and natural beauty.

Background | Culture | Event

Background


The Panagbenga Festival traces its origins back to 1995 when the people of Baguio City sought to rejuvenate their community following the devastating earthquake of 1990. This event marked a turning point, inspiring locals to create a celebration of renewal and resilience. Initially conceived as a one-time event, the festival's success led to its establishment as an annual tradition.


Panagbenga, which means "season of blooming" in the local Kankanaey language, showcases the region's vibrant flora and rich cultural heritage. The festival typically takes place in February, coinciding with the city's mild climate and the flowering season of many local plants. Beyond its aesthetic appeal, Panagbenga holds deep symbolic significance for the people of Baguio City. It serves as a testament to their resilience in the face of adversity and a celebration of the city's natural beauty and cultural diversity.


Today, Panagbenga continues to evolve, incorporating new elements while staying true to its roots. It remains a cherished tradition, beloved by locals and tourists alike, and a source of pride for the entire Baguio community.

BELIEFS

Beliefs surrounding the Panagbenga Festival center on nature reverence and gratitude to land and crop spirits. Residents view it as a time to thank deities for abundant harvests and seek blessings for the year ahead. Some believe celebrating flowers brings prosperity, happiness, and luck.


Panagbenga serves as a platform to preserve and promote regional culture, honoring ancestors and imparting traditions to future generations. These beliefs underscore the festival's significance, fostering a deep connection between people, nature, and cultural heritage. Through rituals and festivities, Panagbenga strengthens bonds within communities and reinforces the importance of environmental stewardship and cultural preservation.

TRADITION

The Panagbenga Festival features rich traditions including vibrant street dancing competitions, elaborately decorated floral floats, and cultural performances showcasing indigenous music and dance. Traditional rituals and ceremonies pay homage to ancestors and seek blessings for the community. Community participation and collaboration are central, fostering unity and pride in cultural heritage. These traditions highlight the Cordillera region's diverse cultural tapestry, promoting social cohesion and preserving indigenous practices for future generations to cherish and celebrate.

MEN'S ATTIRE

During the Panagbenga Festival, men often wear traditional attire inspired by the indigenous culture of the Cordillera region. This may include bahag (woven skirts) or g-strings, typically paired with fabric or leather ornaments and tribal motifs. They may also wear embroidered shirts or woven fabric jackets, often adorned with traditional patterns. Men often complete their attire with traditional hairstyles, such as woven headdresses or decorated bamboo hats.

WOMEN'S ATTIRE

During the Panagbenga Festival, women showcase traditional attire influenced by the indigenous culture of the Cordillera region. This may consist of tapis (woven skirts) or dresses embellished with intricate beadwork and tribal motifs. They often pair these with embroidered blouses or jackets adorned with vibrant patterns. Handmade jewelry, such as beaded necklaces and metalwork bracelets, complements their outfits, highlighting the region's rich cultural heritage.

EVENT

The Panagbenga Festival offers a myriad of captivating activities and events celebrating the beauty and culture of Baguio and the surrounding region. Spectacular street parades feature colorful floats adorned with fresh flowers and traditional dance performances. Visitors can also enjoy folk dance competitions showcasing indigenous dances from the Cordillera region.


Another highlight of the festival is the floral exhibits, where visitors can admire a variety of indigenous flowers and plants beautifully displayed. The Session Road in Bloom event transforms Baguio's main street into a lively floral garden, with food and craft stalls offering local specialties and artisanal products.


At night, the city lights up with dazzling fireworks and light shows that add to the festive atmosphere. Finally, food stalls offering delicious local cuisine are scattered throughout the city, giving visitors the opportunity to savor traditional dishes from the region.

Kinilaw is a popular dish enjoyed by locals and visitors alike. Kinilaw is a traditional Filipino dish similar to ceviche, typically made with fresh raw fish or seafood marinated in vinegar, calamansi juice (a type of citrus fruit), onions, ginger, and chili peppers. The acidity from the vinegar and calamansi juice "cooks" the raw fish, giving it a tangy flavor while preserving its freshness.


Additionally, it is often prepared using locally sourced ingredients, such as fresh fish caught from nearby rivers or seas. The dish is usually served as an appetizer or as part of a larger meal, accompanied by rice or other side dishes. Some variations of kinilaw may include additional ingredients like coconut milk, cucumber, or tomatoes, adding complexity to the flavor profile.
