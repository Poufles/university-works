Treat Yourself to Cordillera's Culinary Gems!

Indulge in the diverse and captivating flavors of Cordillera cuisine, an authentic gastronomic adventure that invites you to embark on a culinary odyssey through the heart of Philippine culture. Immerse yourself in a rich tapestry of taste sensations as you explore a vibrant array of dishes meticulously prepared with fresh, locally sourced ingredients.


Whether you're exploring bustling markets, dining in cozy eateries, or participating in traditional feasts, Cordillera cuisine promises a culinary journey like no other—a celebration of flavors, textures, and aromas that reflect the rich tapestry of Philippine culinary heritage. So come, immerse yourself in the culinary wonders of Cordillera, and let your taste buds embark on an unforgettable voyage of discovery.

Experience a symphony of flavors: Pinuneg, a traditional blood sausage; Etang, a spicy pork stew; Kuniing, a smoked pork sausage; Pinikpikan, a smoked chicken stew; and Kiniwar, a spicy meat salad. Let yourself be swept away by the culinary adventure of the Cordilleras!

Ingredients

  • 1 whole chicken
  • Salt
  • 3 to 4 cloves of garlic, crushed
  • 1 piece of ginger (about 2 inches), peeled and thinly sliced
  • 1 onion, peeled and quartered
  • 2 to 3 green chili peppers (optional)
  • Vegetables of your choice (green beans, potatoes, cabbage, etc.)
  • 2 tablespoons of vinegar
  • Water
  • Seasonings

    • PINIKPIKAN

    A traditional Filipino chicken dish with a unique preparation method that involves beating the live chicken before cooking it. This practice is believed to enhance the flavor and texture of the meat, giving it a distinctive smoky taste. The dish is typically made using native or free-range chicken, which is prized for its robust flavor and firm texture.


    How to Cook

    (Hover on the Photo to see Ingredients)

    1. Start by preparing the chicken, cleaning it thoroughly inside and out. You can keep it whole or cut it into smaller pieces according to your preference.
    2. Rub the chicken with salt inside and out. Let it sit for about 30 minutes to allow the salt to penetrate the meat.
    3. In a large pot, add the chicken, crushed garlic, sliced ginger, onion, green chili peppers (if using), and enough water to completely cover the chicken.
    4. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat and simmer over low heat for about 30 minutes to 1 hour, depending on the size of the chicken, until it is fully cooked and the meat easily falls off the bones.
    5. During cooking, regularly skim off any impurities that rise to the surface.
    6. Once the chicken is cooked, remove it from the pot and let it cool slightly.
    7. Meanwhile, add the vegetables of your choice to the pot and simmer until tender.
    8. Once the chicken has cooled, use your hands to shred the meat into coarse pieces.
    9. Return the shredded chicken to the pot with the vegetables, then add the vinegar. Season with black peppercorns and bay leaves, if desired.
    10. Let simmer for a few more minutes to allow the flavors to meld.
    11. Serve hot and enjoy this delicious traditional dish Pinikpikan!

    PINIKPIKAN

    A traditional blood sausage originating from the mountainous Cordillera region in the Philippines. It is prepared using either pork or chicken blood mixed with lean pork meat, chopped offal (such as liver or heart), garlic, onions, salt, and pepper. This mixture is then stuffed into pork intestines and boiled until cooked through.


    How to Cook

    (Hover on the Photo to see Ingredients)

    1. Mix pork or chicken blood with diced lean pork meat and finely chopped pork offal in a bowl.
    2. Add minced garlic, chopped onions, salt, and pepper to the meat and blood mixture, and mix well to incorporate all ingredients.
    3. Let the mixture rest in the refrigerator for a few hours or overnight to allow the flavors to develop.
    4. Meanwhile, prepare the pork intestines by thoroughly cleaning them and cutting them into appropriate-sized pieces for casing the sausage.
    5. Fill the pork intestines with the meat and blood mixture, making sure not to pack them too tightly to allow the sausage to expand during cooking.
    6. Tie the ends of the sausages with kitchen twine to seal them.
    7. In a pot of boiling water, place the sausages and let them cook for about 30 to 40 minutes, or until fully cooked.
    8. Remove the sausages from the water and let them cool slightly before slicing and serving.

    Ingredients

  • Pork or chicken blood
  • Lean pork meat, diced
  • Pork offal (such as liver or heart), finely chopped
  • Garlic, minced
  • Onions, finely chopped
  • Salt and pepper, to taste
  • Pork intestines (for casing the sausage)

    • Ingredients

  • Choose lean cuts of pork meat, typically from the shoulders or sides.
  • Salt
  • Wood (used to smoke the meat, imparting its characteristic aroma)
  • Open Flame (used to smoke the meat over an extended period)

    • KINIING AT ETAG

    A traditional smoked pork delicacy from the Cordillera region of the Philippines. It is made by thinly slicing lean cuts of pork meat and seasoning them with salt. The seasoned meat is then skewered onto bamboo sticks or rods and smoked over an open flame, typically using Benguet wood for its distinctive aroma


    How to Cook

    (Hover on the Photo to see Ingredients)

    1. Begin by selecting lean cuts of pork meat.
    2. Season the pork meat with salt, ensuring it is evenly coated.
    3. Allow the meat to marinate in the salt for a few hours or overnight in the refrigerator.
    4. Next, arrange the seasoned pork meat on bamboo skewers or sticks, ensuring they are securely pierced through.
    5. Smoke the skewered pork meat over an open flame or using a smoker, rotating them occasionally to ensure even cooking.
    6. Continue smoking the meat until it is fully cooked and has acquired a smoky flavor and a slightly charred exterior.
    7. Once cooked, remove the skewers from the heat and allow the "kiniing" and "etag" to cool slightly before serving.
    8. Slice the smoked pork thinly and serve as a flavorful and savory dish, typically enjoyed with rice and local condiments.

    KINIWAR

    A delicious traditional Filipino dessert. It is made by cooking glutinous rice with coconut milk and sugar until it becomes sticky and caramelized. The mixture is then typically placed in a banana leaf-lined pan and topped with latik, which is a sweet coconut milk residue. The dish is then baked or steamed until the flavors meld together and the top becomes golden brown. Kiniwar/Biko is loved for its sweet, rich flavor and chewy texture, making it a favorite dessert during celebrations and gatherings in the Philippines.


    How to Cook

    (Hover on the Photo to see Ingredients)

    1. Rinse the glutinous rice with cold water until the water runs clear, then drain it.
    2. In a saucepan, combine the glutinous rice, coconut milk, and brown sugar. Optionally, add a pinch of salt to enhance the flavors.
    3. Bring the mixture to a boil over medium heat, stirring constantly to prevent the rice from sticking to the bottom of the saucepan.
    4. Reduce the heat to low and let it simmer, stirring occasionally, until the liquid is absorbed and the rice is cooked and becomes sticky and creamy. This may take about 20 to 25 minutes.
    5. If desired, add butter and vanilla extract to enrich the flavor of the Kiniwar.
    6. Meanwhile, preheat the oven to 180°C (350°F) and lightly grease a baking dish.
    7. Transfer the cooked rice mixture to the baking dish, spreading it evenly.
    8. Bake the Kiniwar for about 20 to 25 minutes, or until the top is golden brown and caramelized.
    9. Once cooked, remove the baking dish from the oven and let it cool slightly.
    10. If desired, garnish the Kiniwar with banana leaves and latik before serving.
    11. Cut the Kiniwar into slices and enjoy it warm or at room temperature as a delicious Filipino dessert.

    Ingredients

  • 2 cups glutinous rice
  • 1 can coconut milk (about 400 ml)
  • 1 cup brown sugar
  • 1 tablespoon butter (optional)
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract (optional)
  • Salt (a pinch, optional)
  • Banana leaves (for lining, optional)
  • Latik (for topping, optional)
